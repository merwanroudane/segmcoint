5. Segmented Cointegration Tests

5.1 Theoretical Background

Standard cointegration tests assume the equilibrium relationship holds over the entire sample. However, it may only hold during certain sub-periods. Kim (2003) formalises this as segmented cointegration:

\(x_{1t} = \alpha + \beta' x_{2t} + \varepsilon_t\)

where the autoregressive root switches between regimes:

\(\rho_t = \begin{cases} \rho < 1 & \text{if } t \in C_T \text{ (cointegration period)} \\ 1 & \text{if } t \in N_T \text{ (non-cointegration period)} \end{cases}\)

14. Economic Interpretation

Key Findings

  1. Standard vs Segmented Cointegration. While the Kim (2003) infimum tests fail to reject the null of no cointegration at conventional levels, the MR (2022) Wald-type tests successfully identify segmented cointegration across all six specifications â€” periods where the equilibrium holds interspersed with temporary breakdowns.
  2. Structural Breaks in Oil Markets. The estimated break dates (1985:01 and 2002:04 for the key raw specifications) correspond to well-known structural shifts: the mid-1980s oil price collapse and the early-2000s commodity super-cycle.
  3. Regime Switching. Under hypothesis H1A, the sample is divided into three regimes: cointegration (1973â€“1985), non-cointegration (1985â€“2002), and cointegration restored (2002â€“2007).
  4. Robustness. Results are broadly robust to the choice of deterministic specification (Cases Iâ€“III) and trimming parameter Îµ âˆˆ [0.10, 0.20].

Policy Implications

The presence of segmented cointegration suggests that long-run relationships between oil market variables are time-varying. Standard full-sample cointegration tests may underestimate the degree of long-run dependence, and forecasting models should account for regime-dependent equilibrium correction mechanisms.
